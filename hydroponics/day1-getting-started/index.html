DAY 1 - The ‘What’ and ‘Why’

So this last spring I was poking around Youtube watching videos on woodworking in hopes of finding that secret trick or tip that would allow me to cut a straight line or measure properly. Well after a lot of searching the only thing I found was a bunch of people that actually make nice stuff and finish their projects – and honestly I don’t need that kind of negativity in my life :). But, the effort wasn’t a complete bust, I did come across some videos on backyard hydroponics! Woo hoo! Another project that’ll let me take useful wood and turn it into useless kindling. But, now with water and electricity added to the mix. What could go wrong?

As with all things that fall under the category of “hey, that looks pretty easy”, there are a gazillion ways to do hydroponics and everyone seems to have an opinion on what’s the best implementation. Luckily for me I’m not particularly interested in hydroponics, I just wanted to build one. I wanted to see if I could get it to work - the whole ‘you get fresh vegetables’ was an afterthought to ‘you get to drink beer and use power tools.’ So, after a lot of research and a diligent application of scientific methods I settled on the nutrient film technique (NFT). Why? Because it looked cool and I got to use PVC glue.

At its essence NFT is just plants grown in some kind of neutral media (rock wool, coconut fiber, etc), jammed into net cups and dangled over some fertilized, aerated water so that their roots are reaching down into the water.

Here’s a really basic diagram:

Pretty straight forward, right?

The only real work you have to put into the system is to pay attention to the water level in the reservoir (do this daily in the summer) and check the pH of the water when adding food (the frequency of feeding varies depending on the types of plants and brand of fetilizer, but figure on doing this every two weeks). You also have to deal with racoons that think it’s okay to pull out the net cups so they can drink from ‘their’ fountain.

As for the current setup itself; off the top of my head the whole thing ran around $200 and took a weekend to put together. I’m sure it could be done cheaper and made to look a lot nicer, but for a first effort it came out okay and it works fairly well.
